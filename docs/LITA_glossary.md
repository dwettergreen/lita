# Life in the Atacama — Glossary
*Authoritative terminology for the LITA website. All public-facing pages should use these definitions on first use of each term.*

---

## Core Project Terms

**Life in the Atacama (LITA)**
The NASA-funded research project (2002–2018) that developed Zoë and conducted five field deployments to Chile's Atacama Desert. Led by Carnegie Mellon University (robotics) and the SETI Institute (science). Funded under NASA's ASTEP and PSTAR programs. Do not confuse with ARADS (a separate follow-on project).

**ARADS (Atacama Rover Astrobiology Drilling Studies)**
A separate NASA project led by NASA Ames that followed LITA. Used the same Honeybee drill technology developed during LITA but with a different robot (K-REX2), not Zoë. Mentioned in the LITA website only under Legacy.

**ASTEP (Astrobiology Science and Technology for Exploring Planets)**
The NASA program that funded the early phases of LITA (approximately 2002–2012). One of the main funding sources for the project.

**PSTAR (Planetary Science and Technology through Analog Research)**
The NASA program that funded later phases of LITA (approximately 2013–2018).

**Zoë**
LITA's autonomous, solar-powered rover, developed at Carnegie Mellon University's Robotics Institute. Four-wheeled, approximately 2.5 m long, 1.9 m wide. Powered by a 3 m² gallium arsenide solar array. Named after the Greek word for "life." *Note the umlaut: Zoë, not Zoe.* Carried a suite of scientific instruments for life detection across all five deployments; equipped with a 1-meter drill from 2015 onward.

**Hyperion**
A predecessor rover used in early project phases (2003 deployment context). Informed the development of Zoë. Larger and less capable of autonomous navigation than Zoë.

**Honeybee Robotics drill**
A 1-meter autonomous drill developed by Honeybee Robotics, integrated onto Zoë for the 2015–2018 deployments. Allowed subsurface soil sampling to 1-meter depth. The same drill technology was later used in the ARADS project.

## Science Terms

**Astrobiology**
The scientific study of the origin, evolution, and distribution of life in the universe — including whether life might exist or have existed on other planets. LITA was an astrobiology project because it developed methods and technology for detecting life, tested in an environment that resembles Mars.

**Biosignature**
Any physical, chemical, or biological evidence that life is or was present. Biosignatures can include living microorganisms, their chemical byproducts, organic molecules, or structures they create. LITA's instruments searched for biosignatures in Atacama soils.
*Plain-language version for use in public text:* "chemical or physical signs of life"

**Microbial life / microorganisms / microbes**
Single-celled organisms too small to see with the naked eye, including bacteria, archaea, and some fungi. The type of life most likely to survive in extreme environments like the Atacama. What LITA was searching for.
*Avoid:* "bugs" (too colloquial), "germs" (implies disease)

**Mars analog**
A place on Earth whose environment resembles Mars in one or more important ways — temperature, dryness, soil chemistry, radiation. The Atacama is a Mars analog because it is extremely dry, has high UV radiation, and has soil chemistry similar to some Martian environments. Testing rovers and instruments in Mars analogs helps prepare for actual Mars missions.

**Hyperarid**
Extremely dry. Technically defined as regions where the ratio of annual precipitation to potential evaporation is below 0.05. The core of the Atacama Desert is hyperarid — some areas receive less than 1 mm of rain per decade.
*Plain-language first use:* "the hyperarid core — regions so dry that less than a millimeter of rain may fall in a decade"

**Habitat / microhabitat**
An environment where an organism lives. In the Atacama, life clusters in microhabitats — small, specific zones with slightly more moisture or protection (under rocks, in salt crusts, near fog zones). LITA mapped the distribution of these microhabitats.

**Distribution of life**
The pattern of where life exists across a landscape. LITA's core scientific question was: in an extremely dry environment, where does life survive, how dense is it, and what environmental factors explain the pattern?

## Technology Terms

**Autonomous navigation / autonomy**
The ability of a robot to move through an environment, avoid obstacles, and follow a planned route without human control at each step. A human operator might define a destination or general path, but the robot decides moment-to-moment how to get there. Critical for Mars missions where communication delays make continuous human control impractical.
*Plain-language version:* "self-driving in the desert"

**Traverse**
A planned route across a landscape, usually a straight or semi-straight path of several kilometers. In field robotics, a traverse is what the rover drives — the path from one point to another. Used in navigation, data collection, and planning.

**Science payload**
The full set of scientific instruments carried by a rover. Each instrument measures something different about the environment. Zoë's payload evolved across deployments.

**Fluorescence imager**
An instrument on Zoë that detects fluorescence — the emission of light by a material after absorbing ultraviolet light. Many biological molecules fluoresce under UV; rocks and minerals generally do not. The fluorescence imager was one of Zoë's primary life-detection tools.
*Plain-language version:* "a specialized camera that makes living things glow under ultraviolet light"

**Spectrometer / spectroscopy**
An instrument that measures the spectrum of light reflected from or emitted by a material. Different materials have characteristic spectral "fingerprints" that reveal their composition. LITA used several types: infrared spectrometers (mineral composition), Raman spectrometers (molecular composition), and others.
*Plain-language version:* "an instrument that identifies materials by analyzing the light they reflect — similar to how a prism splits light into colors, but far more precise"

**Raman spectrometer / Raman spectroscopy**
A specific type of spectrometry that identifies molecular composition using laser-induced light scattering. Useful for identifying minerals and organic compounds. The Mars Microbeam Raman Spectrometer (MMRS) was part of Zoë's payload in later deployments and was also a candidate instrument for the Mars 2020 mission.

**Neutron detector / DAN (Dynamic Albedo of Neutrons)**
An instrument that measures hydrogen abundance in soil — a proxy for water content. Used in early deployments. The radioactive tritium source in the DAN instrument decayed over time, and LITA's experience with DAN informed NASA's planning for Curiosity's identical instrument.

**Panorama / panoramic mosaic**
A wide-angle image created by stitching together many individual photographs. Zoë's cameras captured overlapping images that were computationally combined into panoramas — some covering a full 360 degrees. Panoramas provide a comprehensive view of a location and can be used by scientists to study the landscape.

**Navcam (Navigation Camera)**
A camera used for navigation — helping the rover understand its immediate environment, avoid obstacles, and plan its path. Distinct from science cameras, though imagery from navcams may have scientific value.

**Pancam (Panoramic Camera)**
A high-resolution camera used for scientific imaging of the landscape. Produces the detailed panoramic imagery used for geological and biological analysis.

**Sol**
A Martian day (approximately 24 hours 37 minutes). In planetary rover operations, field days are sometimes counted as "sols" even in terrestrial field tests, aligning with the Mars mission operational convention.

## Place Terms

**Atacama Desert**
A desert in northern Chile extending roughly 1,000 km along the Pacific coast, between the Andes and the coastal mountains. The driest non-polar desert on Earth. Hyperarid in its core. Rich in mineral salts (nitrates, perchlorates, halides) similar to Mars. High UV radiation due to altitude and atmospheric conditions. Periodically subject to fog events near the coast that enable limited life.

**Yungay / Estación Yungay**
A field site in the hyperarid core of the Atacama, considered one of the driest places on Earth. A key location for LITA field deployments.

**Salar Grande / Salt flats**
Large salt flat areas in the Atacama where evaporated ancient lakes left behind thick deposits of halite (rock salt) and other minerals. Sites of extreme aridity; also locations where salt-tolerant microorganisms can sometimes be found.

**Altiplano**
The high-altitude plateau of the Andes that forms the eastern edge of the Atacama region. Higher elevation, different environmental conditions than the lower desert floor. Some LITA field sites were in the Altiplano region.

## Institutions

**Carnegie Mellon University (CMU) Robotics Institute**
Led the rover development and field operations for LITA. Based in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania. David Wettergreen was the robotics PI.

**SETI Institute / Carl Sagan Center**
Led the science investigation for LITA. Nathalie Cabrol was the science PI. Based in Mountain View, California, with affiliation to NASA Ames Research Center.

**NASA Ames Research Center**
NASA center in Silicon Valley, California. Affiliated with the science leadership of LITA.

**Honeybee Robotics**
Engineering company that designed and built the 1-meter autonomous drill integrated onto Zoë for the 2015–2018 LITA deployments.

**Universidad Católica del Norte (UCN)**
Chilean university partner in LITA field operations, based in Antofagasta.

## Archive Terms

**Field deployment**
A period when the LITA team traveled to the Atacama with Zoë to conduct experiments. LITA had five field deployments: 2003, 2004, 2005, 2013, and 2015–2018.

**Image archive**
The complete collection of images captured by Zoë's cameras during field deployments — hundreds of thousands of images across all deployments.

**Data product**
A processed version of raw instrument data, formatted for scientific use. For example, a calibrated spectrum derived from raw spectrometer readings, or a georeferenced panorama with GPS coordinates.

**GIS (Geographic Information System)**
Software and data formats used for working with geographically referenced data. Traverse routes, sample locations, and instrument readings can be mapped using GIS tools. LITA archive data is provided in standard GIS formats (GeoJSON, KML, shapefile) where applicable.
