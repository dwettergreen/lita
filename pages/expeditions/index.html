Field Deployments

Five Expeditions

From Hyperion's first traverse in 2003 to Zoë's autonomous drilling in 2015 — five field seasons spanning two funded phases of research.

Deployment 1 · Phase 1

2003 — Instrument & Navigation Tests

Hyperion rover was deployed to the Atacama in April 2003. The team of 17, based at a site near Iquique, tested instruments including VIS/NIR spectrometer, fluorescence imaging, and autonomous navigation. Led by Wettergreen; team included Urmson, Wagner, Teza, Calderon, Moersch, and others.

Deployment 2 · Phase 1

2004 — Zoë's First Campaign

Zoë rover replaced Hyperion for this August–October deployment. Instruments now included a fluorescence imager, VIS/NIR spectrometer, and stereo panoramic imager. Operated from Sites B and C. Team of ~25 in Chile with a remote science team in Pittsburgh.

Deployment 3 · Phase 1

2005 — First Autonomous Life Map

A landmark result: Zoë became the first robot to autonomously map the distribution of microbial life across a landscape, detecting chlorophyll, DNA, and protein in Atacama soils. Ran August–October 2005 across Sites D, E, and F. Science team operated remotely from Pittsburgh while ~13 engineers and scientists worked in Chile.

Deployment 4 · Phase 2

2013 — First Autonomous Drilling

Zoë returned to the Atacama equipped with the Honeybee Robotics drill and new instruments: MMRS (Mars Microbeam Raman Spectrometer), BUF (Bio-UV Fluorescence Imager), and VNIR spectrometer. Based at Mina Guanaco, June 11–July 1. Found unusual salt-adapted subsurface microbes — the first robot-drilled life detection in the Atacama.

Deployment 5 · Phase 2

2015 — Full Autonomous Traverse

The final deployment, March 26–April 13, 2015. Based at ESO Paranal Observatory south of Antofagasta. Zoë conducted fully autonomous drilling traverses at Sites H and I. Team of 9 engineers and scientists. First time the complete rover-drill-instrument system operated autonomously under a Mars-relevant remote science team scenario.
