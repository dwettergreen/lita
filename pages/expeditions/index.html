Field Deployments

Five Expeditions

From the first autonomous traverse in 2003 to subsurface drilling in 2018 — sixteen years of discovery in one of Earth's most extreme environments.

Deployment 1

2003 — First Traverse

Hyperion and early Zoë hardware were deployed for the first time. The focus was on testing autonomous navigation over long distances and validating individual scientific instruments in the desert environment.

Read More
Deployment 2

2004 — Expanding Range

Zoë's instruments were more tightly integrated and autonomous navigation capabilities extended. The team pushed for longer traverses, targeting 50 km across the hyperarid core, five times the previous year's distance.

Read More
Deployment 3

2005 — First Autonomous Life Map

A landmark result: Zoë became the first robot to autonomously map the distribution of microbial life across a landscape, detecting chlorophyll, DNA, and protein in Atacama soils. Results were presented at the Lunar and Planetary Science Conference.

Read More
Deployment 4

2013 — Going Deeper

Zoë returned equipped with a 1-meter autonomous drill from Honeybee Robotics and new spectrometers including the Mars Microbeam Raman Spectrometer. The team found unusual, highly salt-adapted microbes in the subsurface — organisms unlike anything found at the surface.

Read More
Deployment 5

2015–2018 — Subsurface Life

The final and most comprehensive phase of LITA. Multiple seasons of fieldwork integrating the full science payload with fully autonomous drilling operations. Data from this phase directly informed NASA Mars 2020 mission planning and led to the ARADS follow-on project.
