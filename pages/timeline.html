Phase 1
2002 – 2006 Surface life survey · Autonomous navigation · NASA ASTEP
2002
Milestone

NASA ASTEP Award — project begins

Life in the Atacama funded under NASA's Astrobiology Science and Technology for Exploring Planets program. CMU leads robotics; SETI Institute leads science. PI: David Wettergreen (CMU Robotics Institute); Science PI: Nathalie Cabrol (SETI / NASA Ames). [SOURCE NEEDED: exact date and grant number]

Apr 2003
Field Deployment 1

Atacama Desert — Hyperion rover

First LITA field campaign. The Hyperion rover is deployed in April 2003 to test instruments and navigation. Experiments include: VIS/NIR spectrometer, fluorescence microscopy, fluorescence imaging, stereo image processing, sun tracking, localization, locomotion power, and integrated rover operations. Camp established near Iquique. [SOURCE NEEDED: exact dates, field site GPS, traverse distance]

Field Deployment 2

Atacama Desert — Zoë's first campaign

Zoë replaces Hyperion as the primary platform. Deployed August–October 2004 to Sites B and C. Integrated instruments: fluorescence imager, VIS/NIR spectrometer, stereo panoramic imager, environmental sensors, and experimental instruments. Remote Pittsburgh science team operates in parallel. Aug–Oct 2005
Field Deployment 3 ★

First Autonomous Life Map

Zoë traverses Sites D, E, and F from late August through October 2005. Up to 15 engineers and scientists in Chile; ~17-person science team operates remotely from Pittsburgh in weekly sessions. First robot to autonomously map distribution of microbial life — detecting chlorophyll, DNA, and protein in Atacama soils. [SOURCE NEEDED: total traverse distance, exact detection results]

Scientific result

Life detected and mapped autonomously

  • Chlorophyll, DNA, and protein detected in Atacama soils by Zoë
  • Life distribution shown to be patchy, tied to moisture microhabitats
  • Results presented at Lunar and Planetary Science Conference, March 2005
Chile (up to 15): Wettergreen, Heys, Jonak, Pane, Ramirez, Smith, Teza, Thompson, Tompkins, Vijaykumar, Wagner, Williams + observers (Blatt, Hinds, Kumagai/IEEE, Roberts/NatGeo) Pittsburgh (~17): Cabrol, Coppin, Warren-Rhodes, Weinstein, Piatek, Drake, Grin, Horton, Moersch + others

2011
Milestone

NASA ASTEP Award — Phase 2 begins

New award (NNX11AJ87G) funds "Robotic Investigation of Subsurface Life in the Atacama Desert." Project sometimes referred to as Subsurface Life in the Atacama. Zoë is reconditioned. Honeybee Robotics begins developing the rover-deployed 1-meter drill (third generation Rotary-Percussive drill, following CRUX and IceBreaker). Field Deployment 4 ★

June 11 – July 1, 2013 · Mina Guanaco

Zoë returns to the Atacama with the Honeybee drill and full instrument suite: MMRS (Raman), BUF (UV fluorescence), VNIR spectrometer. Base camp at Mina Guanaco operating gold mine, ~2700m elevation. Two science traverse windows of 5 days each. Team rotation mid-deployment.

Scientific result

Salt-adapted subsurface microbes found

  • First autonomous rover drilling operation in the Atacama
  • Soil samples from ~1 m depth contain unusual, highly salt-adapted microbes
  • Organisms different from surface microbes; relevant to Martian subsurface conditions
  • Published: Frontiers of Microbiology, February 2019

Field Deployment 5 ★

2015–2019
Legacy

Publications and impact

2013 microbial sample analyses published in Frontiers of Microbiology (February 2019). DAN neutron detector results from LITA led NASA to revise the operational timeline for the identical DAN instrument on the Curiosity rover. The Honeybee drill technology was subsequently used in the ARADS project — a separate NASA Ames-led project using the K-REX2 robot, not Zoë.
