The Robot

Meet Zoë

An autonomous, solar-powered rover built to find life where almost nothing survives — and to prove that robots could do the same on Mars.

Anatomy

How Zoë is built

Zoë is about the size of a small car — roughly 2.5 meters long and 2 meters wide — with a flat 3 m² solar array lying across her back like a sunbathing lizard. That array, made of high-efficiency gallium arsenide cells, is her only power source. No gasoline, no battery swap. If the sun shines, Zoë runs.

Her four wheels are each independently driven, and a passive joint at the front axle lets her flex and roll over uneven ground — similar to a wagon hitch. All her computers, electronics, and batteries are sealed in a single body mounted between the axles, protecting them from Atacama dust and temperature swings.

How She Drives

Autonomous navigation

A rover on Mars can't be joysticked in real time. Radio signals take up to 24 minutes to travel one way. Zoë was built to solve this — to drive for kilometers across rough terrain using only what her own cameras could see.

Zoë uses a pair of stereo cameras mounted at the front to build a 3D picture of the terrain ahead. An obstacle-detection algorithm identifies rocks, slopes, and soft soil that could trap or tip the rover. A path planner then finds a safe route toward the target waypoint.

A second camera, pointing downward at an angle, measures optical flow — the apparent movement of the ground as Zoë drives — to estimate her speed and position even without GPS. On Mars there is no GPS network; position must be computed from wheel odometry and camera data combined.

Science Payload

The instruments

Zoë's instruments evolved across five deployments. Each was chosen to detect different signs of life — or the conditions that support it.

Life Detection

Fluorescence Imager

Think black light. The fluorescence imager shines ultraviolet light on a soil sample and photographs what glows back. Living organisms fluoresce under UV in ways that rocks don't. Used in every deployment to scan for biological material along the traverse.

Mineralogy

Spectrometers

A spectrometer identifies materials by the light they reflect — each mineral and organic compound has a distinctive spectral fingerprint. Zoë carried infrared and, in later deployments, Raman spectrometers to characterize soil and rock composition.

Subsurface Access · 2013–2018

The Honeybee Drill

For the final deployments, Zoë gained a one-meter autonomous drill from Honeybee Robotics. The drill bores into the ground and brings soil samples to surface-mounted instruments — LITA's first capability to look for life that's hiding underground.

2013–2018

The Honeybee 1-meter drill

Direct evidence of life on Mars, if it exists, is more likely to be found underground — shielded from UV radiation and temperature extremes by even a few centimeters of soil. LITA's drill was designed to prove that a rover could autonomously bore, sample, and analyze without a human holding the controls.

The drill was developed by Honeybee Robotics and integrated onto Zoë's deck. An auger bit rotates and advances into the ground, pulling soil chips upward into a collection system. The samples are then delivered to the spectrometers and fluorescence imager mounted on the rover.

Before Zoë

Hyperion: the predecessor

Before Zoë, the project used Hyperion — a larger, earlier rover developed at Carnegie Mellon. Hyperion was deployed to the Atacama in the first field season (2003) to test individual scientific instruments and early autonomous navigation software.

Hyperion's field results informed every design decision that went into Zoë. What worked in Pittsburgh didn't always work in a desert at altitude with temperature swings of 40°C between sunrise and midday. Zoë incorporated lessons learned.
