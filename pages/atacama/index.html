The Place

The Atacama Desert

The driest non-polar desert on Earth. The closest thing to Mars we can reach by truck. The setting for 16 years of discovery.

Why Here

A place almost too dry for life

Some parts of the Atacama receive less than one millimeter of rain in a decade. There are weather stations that have never recorded rainfall.

Stretching 1,000 kilometers along Chile's Pacific coast, squeezed between the Andes mountains and the coastal range, the Atacama is hyperarid — technically defined as so dry that even salt-tolerant plants cannot survive in most of it. The interior core has mineral-rich, bone-dry soil chemistry remarkably similar to Mars.

That similarity is why LITA chose it. If Zoë could detect life in the Atacama — where life barely exists and barely hangs on — then the instruments and methods she used could be trusted to look for life on Mars.

The Mars Connection

Why the Atacama resembles Mars

Extreme Dryness

Mars is also hyperarid. If liquid water exists there at all, it's transient or deep underground. The Atacama tests whether life-finding instruments can work in the near absence of water.

Soil Chemistry

Atacama soils are rich in perchlorates, nitrates, and halides — the same mineral salts detected on Mars by the Phoenix lander and Curiosity rover. Life that survives here has adapted to these compounds.

UV Radiation

High altitude and thin atmosphere mean elevated UV radiation in the Atacama — closer to the radiation environment on Mars than most of Earth's surface.
