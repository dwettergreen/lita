The Question

Is there life beyond Earth?

We can't yet send a geologist to Mars. But we can send a robot — one smart enough to navigate rough terrain, aim its instruments, and report back what it finds.

The Life in the Atacama project spent 16 years asking how. Not in a lab, but in the field: deploying a solar-powered rover named Zoë into Chile's Atacama Desert — one of the closest things to Mars we have on Earth — to develop the technologies and techniques that a future life-seeking mission would need.

The Atacama is so dry that some regions receive less than a millimeter of rain in a decade. Life barely hangs on there. If Zoë could find it and map it autonomously, we could do the same on Mars.

The Robot

Meet Zoë

Zoë — named after the Greek word for "life" — is a four-wheeled, solar-powered rover about the size of a small car. Built at Carnegie Mellon University's Robotics Institute, she can navigate rough desert terrain for kilometers at a time without a human telling her which way to turn.

Across five deployments, Zoë carried cameras, spectrometers, a fluorescence imager, and ultimately a one-meter drill — each instrument designed to detect the chemical and physical signs of life in soil and rock.

In 2005, Zoë became the first robot to autonomously map the distribution of microbial life across a landscape. In 2013, she drilled and found unusual salt-adapted microbes a meter underground.

What We Found

Key findings

2005

The first autonomous life map

Zoë became the first robot to autonomously survey and map the distribution of microbial life across a landscape — detecting chlorophyll, DNA, and proteins in Atacama soils without human direction.

2013

Life a meter underground

Using a 1-meter drill, Zoë recovered soil samples from the hyperarid core of the Atacama and found salt-adapted microbes unlike any surface organisms — microbes that may resemble what could survive in the Martian subsurface.

2002–2018

Life is patchy — and that matters for Mars

Across all deployments, LITA showed that life in extreme environments clusters in microhabitats tied to moisture, shade, and soil chemistry. A rover searching Mars must be able to find and recognize those patches.
